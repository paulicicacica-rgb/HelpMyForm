💼 Part-Time Work

Working Part-Time on Jobseeker's Allowance

You can work up to 3 days per week and still receive a reduced Jobseeker's Allowance payment. Working 4 or more days stops the payment entirely. Here is how the calculation works.

⏱ 6 min read · ✓ Updated 2026 · 🇮🇪 Ireland

The 4-day rule

Jobseeker's Allowance is paid for days you are unemployed, subject to a maximum of 6 days per week (Sunday is not counted). You must be unemployed for at least 4 days in any 7-day period to qualify. Working 4 or more days in a week means you do not qualify for JA for that week.

Working up to 3 days per week means you can receive a reduced JA payment for the days you are not working.

How casual earnings are assessed

The daily earnings disregard

The first €20 of daily earnings is fully disregarded. After that, 60% of the remaining daily earnings is assessed as weekly means.

Example — working 1 day at €100 net

Day disregard: €20. Assessable earnings: €100 - €20 = €80. Weekly means from work: €80 × 60% = €48. Maximum JA (single, 25+): €232. Weekly JA payment: €232 - €48 = €184.

Systematic short-time working

If your employer has put you on systematic short-time working — a fixed pattern of reduced days due to the employer's business situation — different rules apply. You may be able to claim JA for the days not worked without it being treated as casual work. Speak to your Intreo centre about your specific situation.

Keeping Intreo informed

You must declare all work and earnings to the DSP. Failing to declare work while claiming JA is considered fraud. The DSP cross-references Revenue earnings data. If you work casually, record all days worked and earnings and declare them honestly at each sign-on.

Always declare workUndeclared work while claiming JA can result in overpayment debts, disqualification, and in serious cases prosecution. The DSP receives earnings information from Revenue. Declaring is always the right approach.
